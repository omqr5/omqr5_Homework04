# History of Snowboarding

Snowboarding hasn’t been around as long as skiing, but it rose has fast and is full of culture.  
Here’s a quick overview.

## Early Origins (1960s–1970s)

### The Snurfer (1965)
- Invented by Sherman Poppen for his daughters  
- Basically a surfboard with a rope  
- Considered the first commercial “snowboard”

### Early Innovators
- Dimitrije Milovich – inspired by surfboards and skateboards  
- Jake Burton Carpenter – designed and built improved boards  
- Tom Sims – skateboarder who helped shape snowboard culture  

## Rise (1980s)
- First real bindings appeared  
- Snowboarding brands formed: Burton, Sims, Winterstick  
- Many ski resorts banned snowboards, seeing them as unsafe  
- Riders had to prove the sport was legitimate

## Mainstream (1990s)
- Resorts start accepting snowboarders  
- Snowboarding enters youth culture; fashion, music, lifestyle  
- Terrain parks developed  
- Companies compete with new tech (sidecut shapes, flex patterns)

## Olympics (1998)
- Snowboarding becomes an Olympic sport in Nagano, Japan  
- Events included halfpipe and giant slalom  
- This solidified the sport globally

## Modern (2000s–Present)
- Big-mountain freeriding explodes  
- Tech innovations:  
  - hybrid profiles  
  - Step-on bindings  
  - Lightweight splitboards  
- Snowboarding spreads worldwide, especially in Japan and Scandinavia  

## Why it Matters
Understanding snowboarding’s roots helps explain:
- Why culture is such a big part of the sport  
- How gear evolved  
- Why certain riding styles exist today
